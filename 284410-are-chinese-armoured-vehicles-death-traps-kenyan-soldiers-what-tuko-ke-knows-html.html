Are Chinese armoured vehicles death traps for Kenyan soldiers? What TUKO.co.ke knows

 Posted on March 13, 2024  |  3 minutes  |  527 words  |  Hettie Henneman

- The government in 2016 purchased Chinese Armoured Personnel Carriers to protect soldiers in the line of duty

- Many of the war vehicles have however been blown up over the years and several soldiers killed while inside

- This is despite an assurance from the government that the vehicles were designed to withstand blasts and save lives

At least five Kenya Defense Forces (KDF) soldiers were on Wednesday, August 29, killed in Lamu East by suspected al-Shabaab militants who blew up the vehicle the soldiers were traveling in using Improvised Explosive Devices (IED).

This was just the latest in a spate of similar attacks that have claimed hundreds of lives of Kenyan soldiers and police officers deployed in the terror-prone regions, raising questions on whether the Chinese Armoured Personnel Carriers the government purchased in 2016 were meant to protect the security officers or condemn them to death.

Reports emerged on Thursday indicating the anti-mines vehicles the government bought from China had all been wiped out by al-Shabaab using IEDs.

"The Chinese Armoured Personnel Carriers (APC) purchased by President Uhuru Kenyatta have all been written off by IEDs and lack of spare parts. Our officers are dying as they patrol in non-armoured Toyota cars," Activist Boniface Mwangi claimed in a tweet seen by TUKO.co.ke on Thursday.

The then National Police Service spokesperson George Kinoti, currently DCI boss, was quoted by Daily Nation in June 2017 saying the government also purchased Mine Resistant Ambush Protected (MRAPs) for protection of soldiers deployed in the terror-prone areas.

According to Kinoti, the MRAPs were built with capability to withstand blasts, whereas the APC were meant to protect the soldiers from splinters fired from explosives and bullets from rifles.

The fighting vehicles, Kinoti emphasised, were able to resist both mine and ammunitions and that the war machines had been tried, tested and approved.

"Depending on the impact, the vehicles can overturn when hit but the officers insder will be safe and ready to fight back," the then Police spokesperson said.

About three years later since the fighting machines arrived in the country from China, not only have they been blown up and reduced into scrap metals, but soldiers and police officers have been killed in the line of duty while using the armoured vehicles.

The al-Shabaab seem to have discovered that the APC could easily be destroyed using IED, so they have been planting explosives on roads targeting the Kenyan soldiers and police officers.
