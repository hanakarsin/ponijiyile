There is a pronounced need for speed in the miraa trade, and to expedite miraa to retailers, full use is made of public service vehicles plying routes throughout Kenya.

This has developed a whole system of specialised miraa transport with Toyota Hilux pick-ups zooming along the routes attaching itself to pre-existing transport options while creating others.

Different destinations require different means of transportation depending on their proximity and other factors like conditions of roads and security according to the miraa drivers.

Speaking on NTV's wicked edition, drivers Isaac Kuria and Justus Munene demystified the belief that they were unlearned, reckless drivers and always driving under the influence.

Munene, a driver for Gunen Miraa Transporters, said not everybody could drive a vehicle assigned to transport the commodity due to the top driving skills needed.

"We do not overspend. We try to maintain maximum speed. If it is 100km/h, you do so for a longer period and not alternating speeds. You maintain a constant speed," he explained.

Kuria from Kush Miraa Transporters pointed out that, despite the speed, they are always keen not to put lives at risk by reducing speeds while crossing busy towns.

The drivers argued they do high speed because miraa, as a perishable commodity, can not be kept in the vehicle for long, and the speeds help them harvest winds, which keeps them fresh.

According to Munene, one must be passionate about driving a manual vehicle and have a sober mind to become a miraa driver.

The drivers pointed out they cause fewer accidents on the roads than other drivers because they are always focused on a specific goal.

They further noted that for a regular driver to be picked to drive vehicles transporting miraa will take at least one year to acquire the top speed driving skills needed to work in the industry.

"We will first train you to drive a manual vehicle without making a lot of noise on the road. We teach you how to handle speeds and negotiate corners. It is like the disciplined forces," they said.

The drivers noted that among them, there were doctors, engineers, ex-forces officers, people with good grades and skills who drive the vehicles out of passion.

They said their greatest threats on the roads are youngsters who drive Subaru and Probox vehicles, boda boda operators, and donkeys.

Kuria and Munene further asked the Kenyan government to open the Somalia market to benefit the entire economy.
