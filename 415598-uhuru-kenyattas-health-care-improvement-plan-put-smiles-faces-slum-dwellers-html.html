Kenya’s health sector has faced a turbulent journey with a myriad of challenges touching on health infrastructure, personnel and drugs.

This has seen the sector bedevilled by the harsh realities of deteriorating facilities and services over the years.

However, President Uhuru Kenyatta recently embarked on a progressive restructuring programme to improve the highly sensitive sector, a move that is seeing cartels being edged out and key infrastructural initiatives undertaken to bring health centres closer to informal settlements.

This is evident in the 2021/22 budget in which KSh 121.1 billion was allocated to the health sector. Out of this amount, 42.2% (KSh 56 billion) will go to development expenditure while KSh 65 billion is reserved for recurrent expenditure.

With a key focus on medical infrastructure targeting the informal settlements, numerous projects have been completed and others are currently underway.

24-hospitals project

Among the highlights is the 24-Hospitals Project, a government initiative involving the development of hospitals in informal settlements that are being rolled out in collaboration with the Nairobi Metropolitan Services.

As part of the project in Nairobi, 19 facilities are being built at a cost of KSh 70 million each. Another five are to undergo rehabilitation at a cost of Sh300 million.

The areas targeted are Viwandani, Majengo, Mathare, Kayole, Soweto, Korogocho, Kawangware, Gitare Marigu, Mukuru Kwa Njenga, Mukuru kwa Reuben, Kibera and Githurai 44. Of the 19 under construction, nine will be Level Three hospitals while 10 will be Level Two hospitals.

Back in August 2020, President Kenyatta commissioned the construction of three hospitals in the informal settlements of Uthiru, Kibera and Mukuru Kwa Reuben, a move that gave the project momentum.

In part of a grand plan towards the transformation of informal settlements, the Level Three hospitals were allocated KSh 210 million. This amount would cater for a 16-bed capacity hospital at Muthua in Uthiru and Kianda, with a bigger 24-bed capacity health facility at Maendeleo Village in Mukuru kwa Reuben.

These are areas where there has been limited access to health, with many residents facing agonizing trips to faraway hospitals while others are forced to grapple with costly private clinics that were at times not licensed to operate.

Affordable health care in slums

Key to note is that for every hospital facility, there will be a subsequent absorption of healthcare workers, catering staff and other casual workers to fully operationalize the facility. Moreover, larger hospitals like Kenyatta National Hospital, Pumwani, Mama Lucy and Mbagathi are likely to be relieved of the pressure currently experienced.

The end result will be the efficient and affordable provision of health care services and facilities to Kenyans.

The next hospital facility that is set to open is the Mathare-Korogocho facility that will see the absorption of over 300 workers and includes facilities such as a dialysis centre, patient admissions ward, an Intensive Care Unit (ICU), pharmacy, theatres and a morgue. The facility will cater for the residents of Korogocho and Mathare.

The developments are likely to reinforce the implementation of the Universal Health Coverage, which has been allocated KSh 47.7 billion to fund its programmes this year.

To better align with development projects in the health sector, more money has been pumped into vaccination, sanitation and treatment.

The government has also set aside KSh 14.3 billion for the purchase of COVID-19 vaccines.

This approach by the government is keenly focusing on ensuring healthcare services are efficient, accessible and affordable to Kenyans.

Additionally, KSh 47.7 billion has also been allocated to deal with the cancer burden by further bringing services closer to the people. Noticeably, Kenyatta University Teaching, Referral and Research Hospital received KSh 450 million for the acquisition of CyberKnife Radiotherapy equipment.

Two cancer centres will also be established in Kakamega and Meru at a cost of KSh 350 million. Other hospitals allocated funds to facilitate the treatment of cancer patients are the Kenyatta National Hospital and the Moi Teaching and Referral Hospital, which have been allocated KSh 15.2 billion and KSh 11.5 billion respectively.

This focus ultimately addresses the pressing concern of health facilities and services that will undoubtedly have far-reaching positive effects on Kenyans.
